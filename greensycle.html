GreenSycle


An interaction design project.
Role: User researcher, UX/UI designer, prototyper

With this design project, my goal was to address the problems from incorrect recycling practices that can arise when the information and regulations of Ann Arbor’s curbside recycling program are not well known. I hope to better inform residents of what can and cannot be recycled, and to also suggest means for the collective recycling of items that are not able to be placed in convenient curbside bins (such as plastic bags, thermal papers, and certain types of packing materials).

Problem Statement:


Prompt: Think of a design solution that can improve your community or day-to-day life.

In a recent article for the New York Times, Livia Albeck-Ripka describes a behavior that waste managers have begun to call wishful or "aspirational recycling," which occurs when "we want something to be recyclable, so we put it in the recycling bin." This unintentional sabotage of recycling initiatives is quite detrimental, as contaminated or impure batches of recycled materials are often rejected by buyers and sent to the landfill instead.

Personas


6 total (including 1 anti-persona):

I created personas based on user research conducted around Ann Arbor's Kerrytown neighborhood. The insights gathered from these interviews with students and other residents informed elements of my design process and helped me narrow my scope and purpose.

Solution Overview


GreenSycle is an Ann Arbor-specific mobile app that serves as a direct connection to ​Recycle Ann Arbor.​ The application makes it fast and easy to search up proper methods of recycling (curbside, drop-off, etc.) in order to cut down on contaminated batches of recycled material. It is a simple, powerful, and informative way to find out how best to manage what you want to - or think you can - recycle in your daily life.

Aspirational recyclers will be able to find basic information on how to properly dispose of materials they are unsure of, based on local standards. The application will also provide details on why the acceptable method is the way it is, and what alternatives to recycling are available for that specific material or it (i.e., reusing glass jars as decorative vases or canning jars).

Application Features


Text & Image Search:

The user will be taken to a result page that displays the name of the material/object and any other identifying specifics; it also indicates whether or not the item is curbside recyclable via colour (green vs. red) and text. The emphasis on curbside recyclability was chosen because of its popularity of use amongst residents of Ann Arbor, as well as at the University of Michigan, in which many buildings provide recycling and trash bins in the hallways.

Calendars & Events:

Users have access to an in-app calendar feature that allows for reminders to be set for trash/recycling collection days. Additionally, upcoming events such as community drop-offs to the recycling center, or neighborhood environmental activities (loosely related to the application), can also be displayed.

Neighborhood:

The application’s distinguishing feature can be accessed inside the main menu. From the map that opens, users can view their current location, as well as the relative locations of other Ann Arbor residents who are using the app. By clicking on the dropped points on the nap, the user can create their own neighborhood events (such as drop-offs), or check if any of their neighbors are planning something for the future.
